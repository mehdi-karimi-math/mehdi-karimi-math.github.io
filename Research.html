Operations of Complex Systems

My current research focuses on the operations of complex systems, with a special emphasis on modern power networks and healthcare analytics. These systems are increasingly interconnected, high-dimensional, and data-rich, requiring scalable optimization algorithms and machine learning techniques to achieve efficiency, resilience, and cost-effectiveness.

In our NSF-funded project, I lead a team of four graduate students to design graph-based distributed optimization algorithms for managing large-scale, renewable-integrated power networks. This includes intelligent network partitioning, decentralized decision-making, and developing robust algorithms that improve system reliability and adaptability under evolving energy paradigms.

In parallel, through a collaboration with OSF HealthCare, I co-lead an applied project with three faculty members and six students, focusing on predicting healthcare claim counts under the Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) using machine learning and deep learning. We work with real-world Social Determinants of Health (SDOH) data, addressing challenges like high missingness and feature integration to uncover key drivers of healthcare costs and improve risk modeling.

Convex Optimization & Domain-Driven Form

Convex optimization plays a central role in my research, bridging theoretical elegance with practical applications across data science, signal processing, control theory, and robust decision-making. A major focus has been developing efficient infeasible-start primal-dual interior-point methods for optimization problems expressed in the Domain-Driven Form, where feasible regions are described via self-concordant barrier functions.

These algorithms form the foundation of my open-source solver, Domain-Driven Solver (DDS), which provides robust certificates of approximate optimality, infeasibility, and unboundedness. Domain-Driven Solver (DDS)

We have released DDS 2.2, which supports a wide range of constraints, including LP, SDP, quantum entropy, and hyperbolic programming. DDS is designed to be extensible; by providing a new self-concordant barrier, researchers can seamlessly add new constraint sets. DDS also demonstrates significant performance improvements over traditional reformulations.

Quantum Entropy and Quantum Relative Entropy

DDS supports constraints involving quantum entropy (QE) and quantum relative entropy (QRE), enabling efficient solutions for quantum information processing applications. DDS outperforms SDP-based approximations and continues to evolve to handle larger, more complex QRE problems.

Current efforts focus on proving a conjectured self-concordant barrier for the epigraph of the QRE function and improving derivative evaluation for matrix-based barrier functions.

Hyperbolic Programming

Hyperbolic programming connects optimization with sum-of-squares methods and the generalized Lax conjecture. DDS supports hyperbolic constraints expressed in multiple formats: monomial lists, straight-line programs, and determinantal representations.

Ongoing work includes designing automatic differentiation techniques for efficiently computing derivatives of hyperbolic polynomials within DDS, leveraging ideas from machine learning to scale computations.

Sum-of-Squares and SDP Complexity

I investigate the Turing machine complexity of semidefinite programs (SDPs) through the theory of sum-of-squares (SOS) polynomials

Beyond theory, SOS methods have driven advances in approximation algorithms and machine learning, including sparse recovery and dictionary learning. This line of work bridges deep mathematical structures with modern algorithmic demands.
